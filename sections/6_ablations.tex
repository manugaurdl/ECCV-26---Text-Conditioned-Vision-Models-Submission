\section{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

We ablate the key architectural and training decisions in \model{} to isolate the contribution of each component.
Unless stated otherwise, all experiments use DINOv2 ViT-B/14 as the frozen visual backbone and RoBERTa-Large as the text encoder, trained for 500k iterations on the full data mixture (\cref{sec:data}).
We evaluate on four metrics: fine-grained classification probe accuracy (FG-CLS), text steerability via CORE retrieval (\cref{subsec:cond_ret_exp}), semantic segmentation mIoU on ADE20k, and personalized object discrimination PR-AUC on PODS with descriptive text prompts (\cref{subsec:text_density_exp}). \MG{should we keep class, supercat?}

\paragraph{Early vs.\ Late Fusion.}
Our method interleaves gated cross-attention blocks within the ViT layers (early fusion); the alternative appends them after the final ViT layer, 
% conditioning on already-frozen features 
adding text information to the ViT output features via the residual stream 
(late fusion).
Late fusion achieves comparable steerability (93.4) and better preserves FG-CLS (86.5 vs.\ 77.0), but degrades performance on PODS by 10 points (45.3 vs.\ 55.3) when conditioning on descriptive text prompts.

Early fusion produces richer multimodal features: by conditioning intermediate layers, text reshapes how the ViT processes visual information rather than merely decorating the final representation. \MG{bleh}

\paragraph{Role of Gating.}
Each cross-attention block uses a zero-initialized tanh gate (\cref{eq:gated_ca}), starts as the frozen ViT at initialization and gradually incorporates text.
Removing gating collapses both steerability (28.4, below the frozen baseline of 43.5) and FG-CLS (36.2), confirming that unconstrained cross-attention catastrophically interferes with frozen features.

\paragraph{Language Projector.}
A two-layer MLP projects RoBERTa embeddings into the ViT's feature space prior to cross-attention (\cref{sec:arch}).
Replacing it with a linear projector degrades FG-CLS by 5.5\% and PODS by 3.8\%  while steerability remains comparable (94.2 vs.\ 93.4).
MLP's expressivity aids with representation alignment, allowing the cross-attention layers to focus more on contextualizing visual features with text rather than bridging the modality gap. \MG{fair statement?}

\paragraph{Role of FFN.}
Each gated cross-attention block includes a similarly gated feed-forward network applied after cross-attention (\cref{eq:gated_ca}).
Removing FFN yields comparable steerability (95.9) and ADE20k (55.1) performance  , with a modest drop on PODS (53.4 vs.\ 55.3).
We retain FFN because it provides consistent gains on fine-grained discrimination; as shown in \cref{tab:cross_backbone}, this gap widens for backbone families with weaker self-supervised features. \MG{to do}

\subsection{Training Duration}
\label{subsec:training_duration}

Steerability emerges rapidly: within 100k iterations, CORE accuracy reaches 85.5 (vs.\ 43.5 for frozen DINOv2), while FG-CLS degrades only modestly to 82.8.
However, tasks that require deeper language understanding continue improving with longer training: between 100k and 500k iterations, PODS improves from 35.0 to 55.3 (+58\%) and RefCOCOg from 58.8 to 71.5 (+25\%), even as steerability saturates by 300k iterations at 92.5\%.
This pattern holds for both early and late fusion (late-fusion PODS: 39.1 at 300k vs.\ 45.3 at 500k), suggesting that longer training instills richer multimodal representations rather than merely teaching the model to route attention.
We adopt 500k iterations for all final models.

The advantage is most pronounced for MAE, where early fusion more than doubles late-fusion steerability (62.6 vs.\ 35.5) and improves PODS by 5.3 points.
For SigLIP, early fusion boosts accuracy by 7.4\% in steerability (90.9 vs.\ 83.5) and 8.3\% in PODS (23.0 vs.\ 14.7).

DINOv2 produces the strongest steerable representations overall, consistent with its richer self-supervised features; SigLIP and MAE both benefit from text conditioning but start from weaker baselines.
