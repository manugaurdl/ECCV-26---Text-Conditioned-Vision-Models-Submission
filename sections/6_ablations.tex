\paragraph{Role of Gating.}
Each cross-attention block uses a zero-initialized tanh gate (\cref{eq:gated_ca}), starts as the frozen ViT at initialization and gradually incorporates text.
Removing gating collapses both steerability (28.4, below the frozen baseline of 43.5) and FG-CLS (36.2), confirming that unconstrained cross-attention catastrophically interferes with frozen features.

\subsection{Training Signal}
\label{subsec:training_signal}
\MG{motivate with two ways to do localization, pointing and segmentation}
The training objective (\cref{sec:objective}) supervises each token with a soft probability distribution derived from the ground-truth segmentation mask, distributing probability uniformly over all tokens that overlap the referent.
We compare this against a natural alternative: a Gaussian heatmap centered on the bounding-box midpoint ($\sigma{=}1.1$), which concentrates all probability mass around a single spatial location regardless of object extent.

The two signals encode fundamentally different inductive biases.
The Gaussian target trains the model to \emph{point}: it rewards collapsing attention to a unimodal peak, discouraging distributed activations across the object.
The segmentation-mask target trains the model to \emph{cover}: it rewards activating every token that overlaps the referent, teaching token-level region matching rather than centre prediction.

This difference produces a striking emergent behavior at inference.
When multiple instances of the queried class appear in an image (e.g., four apples), the Gaussian-trained model activates around a single instance, consistent with its unimodal supervision.
The mask-trained model, by contrast, activates on \emph{all} semantically matching regions simultaneously, despite never seeing multi-instance annotations during training.
This generalization arises because the mask signal teaches the cross-attention layers to identify \emph{which tokens match the text description} rather than \emph{where the most likely centre is}; at inference, any token whose content matches the query receives high activation, regardless of how many such tokens exist.
This region-matching inductive bias directly supports \model{}'s goal of producing steerable representations rather than task-specific localization outputs.

\subsection{Training Duration}
\label{subsec:training_duration}

Steerability emerges rapidly: within 100k iterations, CORE accuracy reaches 85.5 (vs.\ 43.5 for frozen DINOv2), while FG-CLS degrades only modestly to 82.8.
However, tasks that require deeper language understanding continue improving with longer training: between 100k and 500k iterations, PODS improves from 35.0 to 55.3 (+58\%) and RefCOCOg from 58.8 to 71.5 (+25\%), even as steerability saturates by 300k iterations at 92.5\%.
This pattern holds for both early and late fusion (late-fusion PODS: 39.1 at 300k vs.\ 45.3 at 500k), suggesting that longer training instills richer multimodal representations rather than merely teaching the model to route attention.
We adopt 500k iterations for all final models.
