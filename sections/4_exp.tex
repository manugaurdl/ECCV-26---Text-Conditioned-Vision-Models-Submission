\subsection{Baselines}
We benchmark SHIFT against a number of existing methods that allow for prompt-based textual steering of their internal vision representations as well as the (potentially unimodal) base models underlying our method. 

For \textbf{late-fusion dual-encoders} (e.g., CLIP~\cite{radford2021_clip}, SigLIP~\cite{zhai2023_siglip}), images and prompts are embedded independently using their respective encoders, limiting multimodal interaction to a simple post-hoc fusion, where the resulting global or dense image embeddings are combined with the text representations via element-wise addition. 
As with the considered \textbf{vision-only baselines}, global feature aggregation is conducted as intended by the base method (e.g., CLS or mean pooling). FLAIR~\cite{xiao2025flair} produces localized image-level representations by applying text-conditioned attention pooling on vision tokens.
We extract dense text-conditioned vision representations from \textbf{MLLMs} by concatenating the user prompt $t$ followed by the image $I$ into a multimodal input sequence and selecting the last-language-block states at image-token positions. For global features, we adapt the last-token pooling approach of E5-V~\cite{Jiang2024E5VUE} and append a postfix instructing the model to summarize the image contents in one word while focusing on the aspect specified by $t$.
Text-conditioned representations are obtained from \textbf{open-vocabulary grounding and segmentation methods} via the modality-fused encoder states, ignoring decoder components responsible for generating bounding boxes or segmentation mask. 
Specifically, with SAM2~\cite{ravi2024sam2}, we take the patch-level outputs of the \textit{Fusion Encoder} and apply mean-pooling to get image-level features. 
Similarly, we use the outputs of the cross-modal \textit{Feature Enhancer} layer of GroundingDINO~\cite{liu2023groundingdino}. However, as features are available across multiple spatial scales, we first interpolate them to a common intermediate feature scale before being averaged to get dense pseudo-patch-level features.

Where supported by the underlying architecture, we process images at a resolution of $336\times336$. For models with non-configurable input dimensions, we still resize to this resolution prior to processing to ensure comparable levels of visual information.

To validate our hypothesis that our method can steer the semantics of the global feature of the ViT, we introduce the task of Conditional Retrieval. - We use Sun dataset comprising different scenes. We create two Categories Indoor and Outdoor each containing 3 classes - bathroom, kitchen, living room and park, residential area, and street. 
- Each class has 500 unique images
- We use Flux Image Editing model to inpaint scene relevant objects in each image (Vase in Living Room, Mug in Kitchen etc.)
- For each class, we impaint 5 scene relevant objects in 100 images respectively.
- The task is one vs all retrieval. Within a class, given an image with impainted object A (for ex. Mug in Kitchen), the task is to retrieve an image of a Kitchen  with a Mug in it. For each image as query, Retrieval database is the 499 images of same class i.e Kitchen. Successful retrieval is if the model retrieves an image with the same impainted object i.e Kitchen with Mug present. 
- Metrics is accuracy.
- Random chance is around 20\%, since for each query there are 99/499 correct images. 
- For models that accept text query, retrieval is done by using the class of the impainted object as text query (for ex. Mug).
- Given text conditioning, Successful retrieval implies the visual representation is steerable with text. The model can shift the semantics of the global representation from the scene (Kitchen) to a text contextualized one (Mug in Kitchen). 

\subsubsection{Standard vision encoders encode the image via the loudest concept in the image}

Figure~\ref{fig:cond_ret_baselines} illustrates the effectiveness of our method over standard ViT baselines.
Query-agnostic vision encoders, whether unimodel (MAE, DinoV2) or cross-model (CLIP, SigLIP), struggle to retrieve the correct image (one with same impainted object) with MAE barely outperforming random guess. 
DinoV2 doubles MAE's performance, due to having more object-centric representations 
CLIP and SigLIP despite having a multimodal space are unable to contextualize its visual features with text with post-hoc late fusion. 
SteerViT, on the other hand, achieves 92.3\% accuracy, demonstrating the effectiveness of our method  in steering the semantics of the visual features. 
This validates our hypothesis that the query-agnostic visual encoders simply collapses to the loudest concept in the image. 
In contrast, our method enables steering of these features with text query resulting in conditional representations that perform perfect retrieval, achieving 96.6\% and 91.3\% accuracy on indoor and outdoor scenes respectively. 

Figure~\ref{fig:cond_ret_baselines} also reveals that our representations are steerable with text. The text query is effective in guiding the semantics of the visual representation. This is illustrated by the catastrophic degradation in retrieval performance when conditioning on a random class instead of the correct class.

\subsubsection{Are MLLMs steerable?}

We evaluate two vision-language representations - one we get from Multimodal LLMs and an intermediate representation that we extract from Open Vocab Localization models (GroudingDino and SAM3).
We find MLLMs are steerable but inefficient. InternVL3-1B scores only 51.2\%, however scaling the LLM by a billion parameters boosts performance to 77\%. Our method, when using the same visual encoder (SigLIP), outperforms their 1B and 2B models by 40\% and 14\% respectively with only additional 130M parameters via cross attention blocks.
This speaks positively about our approach. Our method can be viewed as an inverted MLLM, where in we condition the visual features on text input instead of conditioning the LLM on visual input. We are in a way doing early fusion, we fuse text features early on within the ViT layers itself, whereas MLLM fuses text after getting frozen visual features.
The efficiency and efficacy of our method highlights the effectiveness of early fusion over late fusion. Because text shapes the visual representation learning process itself, it can guide attention towards the salient visual regions unlike late fusion. Recent work (cite https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08008) has shown that VLMs perform substantially worse than their visual encoder across a series of vision-centric benchmarks, which also corroborates our findings.

A consequence of the foreground bias in current visual encoders is that these systems struggle with dense predictions for non-salient visual concepts such as background objects.
In the previous section, we validated the primary desideratum of text conditioned visual representations, steering global semantics. In this section, we explore another desirable property of dense routing attention via tokens containing object of interest.
To test this, we take object centric images from PASCAL VOC dataset, and stitch them together to form a mosaic of 4 images. This reduces saliency of each object, so objects aren't necessarily in the foreground.
This enables us to qualitatively analyze and quanitfy the foreground bias of self-attention.

\subsubsection{Qualitative Analysis}
We qualitatively analyze the attention maps of DinoV2 <CLS>. We find that DinoV2 attends to salient objects in the foreground, in essence capturing the loudest concept in the image as we hypothesized earlier (cite). In contrast, our text informed representations are able route significant information through the tokens comprising the query visual concept. This is illustrated in Figure 2, where DinoV2 allocates computation towards the house, bathtub but not to the tree on the road. In contrast, our method which conditions DinoV2 with “tree”, is able effectively attend to the entire tree and even focus partly on the potted plant in the bathroom due to it semantic association. We provide multiple such qualitative examples in the Appendix.

\subsubsection{Quanitfying Dense Prediction.}
We quantify the vision encoder's ability to localize distinct objects using their attention maps as a proxy. We utilize the object segmentation masks from PASCAL-VOC and compute the Area under the curve between DinoV2 <CLS> tokens and ground-truth masks for the queried object. 
We report it in Figure~{\ref{fig:steer_attn}} , we find that text conditioned features significantly outperform query-agnostic general purpose features (by 33.7\%) for fine-grained dense prediction tasks. 

In Figure~\ref{fig:mosaic_baselines}, although visual encoders focus primarily on salient objects, the intermediate features of GroundingDino and SAM3 can be steered with text towards objects of interest. This makes sense since they optimize over the frozen visual encoders for localization. 
Although these models satisfy the first two desiderate for text conditioned visual features, they fail to preserve the final desired propety - i.e preserving representation quality of the frozen visual features as illustrated by Figure~{\ref{fig:teaser}}. We analyze this further in the next section.

\subsection{Desideratum 3: Preserving Visual Representation Quality while Steering}

Although, we emphasize the importance of steerability and query-dependent visual features, the primary purpose of visual representation learning is to produce visual features that transferrable to different downstream tasks such as classification, segmentation etc. 
Obtaining task-relevant visual representations requires the ability to steer features based on objects of interest. However, this must not come at cost of degradation of the visual features. 
A trivial solution, completely overwriting visual features with text features, yields total steerability but completely discards the underlying visual representation.
Although SAM3 and GroundingDINO are able to steer the visual features as shown in Section~{\ref{}}, they fail to preserve the representation quality of the visual features as illustrated by Figure~{\ref{teaser}}.

Hence, existing base models typically fall into three cateogries:

\textbf{High Steerability, Low Quality}: Models like sam3 and groundingDINO are heavily steerable via text but exhibit poor overall feature quality on classification and segmentation probes.

\textbf{Mediocre Steerability, Mediocre Quality}: Multimodal LLMs perform well on classification probes but falter on dense tasks, anb they exhibit decent steerability if the LLM is scaled to 2 billion parameters.

\textbf{Low Steerability, High Quality}: Standard vision encoders like DINO and SigLIP provide rich visual features but lack text-steerability.

In contrast, our approach bridges this gap, achieving high steerability and relatively less degradation (10\%) on classification and segmentation as shown in Figure~{\ref{fig:teaser}}.

Interestingly, we can circumvent this degradation by manually modulating how much cross attention blocks contextualize the visual features with text. 

\subsubsection{Controlling the Visual Quality - Steerability Tradeoff with a Knob}

We contextualize text information within the visual features using cross-attention blocks that are tanh-gated (Eq.~\ref{eq:gated_ca}).
Because these gated blocks are zero-initialized, our model is mathematically identical to DINO at initialization (gate factor = 0.0). Through training the model learns to incorporate text features to steer its visual representation.
Interestingly, the gate can act as a continuous control "knob," allowing us to explicitly manage the tradeoff between feature quality and steerability.
By scaling the gate magnitude, we can smoothly nudge the representations between their original DINO subspace and a text-conditioned state.
Plotting this trajectory (the green curve in Figure 1) reveals a clear Pareto frontier. 
Progressively scaling the gate factor improves steerability. Notably, we observe an optimal operating point at a scaling factor of 0.6. 
At this setting, the model largely preserves the foundational feature quality of DINO while successfully unlocking high text-steerability, outperforming both standard vision encoders and models like SAM3 and GroundingDINO.

Figure~{\ref{fig:mosaic_gate_factor}} also illustrates that how increasing the gate from 0.0 (model is identical to DinoV2) to 0.6, performance on mosaic benchmark doubles from 0.15 to 0.34, demonstrating the model is able to route attention through tokens of interest.

In Section~{\ref{sec:exp}}, we saw that what we condition on has a profound impact on the resultant feature quality, with performance dropping by half when conditioning on a random object class. In this section, we dive deeper into the role of text conditioning in determining the resultant visual feature.
We evaluate our model on the Personal Object Discrimination Suite (PODS), to test the ability of our method in encoding personalized objects under distribution shifts. Our experiments reveal that the semantics of the resultant representation is highly sensitive to the specificity of the text prompt.
We observe that the model does not simply ``add'' information to the DINOv2 baseline; rather, it steers the feature space to match the resolution of the prompt.

\subsubsection{Text Specificity guides Semantic Abstraction}
To better understand semantic steering of the features, we analyze performance across three levels of textual details in Figure 3:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Coarse (Supercategory):} When conditioning on high-level supercategories, the model discards lower-level texture or shape cues that are irrelevant to the superclass but crucial for instance identity. This explains the performance drop compared to vanilla DINOv2, which retains these features by default.
  \item \textbf{Intermediate (Class):} Conversely, conditioning on ill described class names (which capture category and some attribute information) significantly improves performance over the baseline.
  \item \textbf{Fine-grained (Descriptive):} The most substantial gains are observed when conditioning on highly descriptive text queries (generated either manually or synthetically). This allows the model to perform instance-level discrimination in a zero-shot manner, surpassing classification accuracy of a fine-tuned supervised DINOv2. Hence, detailed prompts act as a precise steering mechanism.
\end{itemize}

This demonstrates the effectiveness and generalizability of our method in using natural language for steering features in out-of-distribution domains with limited data available for finetuning.

\subsection{Visualizing Semantic Control via t-SNE}
\label{sec:tsne_viz}

We have shown that our method can steer the semantics of the global ViT features (Section~\ref{subsec:cond_ret_exp}), preserve the original visual feature quality (Section~\ref{subsec:mosaic_exp}) and that specificity/density of the text query directly influences where the feature lies on the hierarchy of semantic abstraction (Section~\ref{subsec:text_density_exp}).
In this section, we use t-SNE visualizations to analyze the embedding space topology to better understand the dynamic steering of visual representations in SteerViT.

Standard pretrained encoders like DINOv2 exhibit a fixed topology dictated by saliency bias and training objectives, typically clustering images based on the dominant foreground object. 
In contrast, our method aims to reshape this topology dynamically based on the input text query. 
We explore this capability through two distinct steering scenarios: \textit{Hierarchical Semantic Steering} and \textit{Compositional Attribute Steering}.
We also analyze how changing text density and scaling down cross attention gating affects this.


\subsubsection{Hierarchical Semantic Steering}

This experiment tests the model's ability to steer clustering based on broad semantic superclasses (vehicles) instead of object level classes (cars, bikes etc.). 

\paragraph{Setup.} We utilize a subset of images containing N distinct object classes divided into k supercategories.

\paragraph{Analysis.} 
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne_semantic}, the baseline DINOv2 embeddings (left) form six distinct, separated clusters. The model rigidly adheres to the granular object identity, treating ``dog'' and ``horse'' as distinct concepts.

However, steering our model with prompt \texttt{``Animal''} (right) transforms the embedding space resulting in:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Animal Manifold}, where dogs, cat, horse, bird, and sheep collapse into a single broad cluster,
    \item \textit{Non-Animal Cluster} that is pushed away from the query-relevant space.
\end{enumerate}
Notably, within the animal macro-cluster, object level semantics are preserved. This is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne_semantic}, where dogs and cats are closer neighbors than birds. 
This validates our hypothesis that our method controls the semantics of clustering while preserving \sout{object level representation structure}.

\subsubsection{Compositional Attribute Steering}

This experiment tests a more challenging premise: Ignoring the primary semantic category of an object (bike is a vehicle while chair is a type of furniture)  and clustering visually dissimilar objects (office chair and bike) based on the presence of a shared object part (wheel).

\paragraph{Setup.} We curate a heterogeneous dataset of images split into two compositional groups:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Has-Part ``Wheel'':} Cart, wheelchair, bike, car, airplane.
    \item \textbf{No-Part ``Wheel'':} Apple, house, person.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Analysis.} 
The standard DINOv2 encoder fails to capture this shared attribute. The t-SNE visualization (Figure~\ref{fig:tsne_compositional}, Left) shows clusters organized purely by object identity (e.g., all houses together, all people together). There is no ``wheel'' axis; for instance, a wheelchair is often clustered closer to a person due to contextual bias than to a cart.

Upon conditioning with the text \texttt{``Wheel''}, our representation effectively ``steers'' away from the dominant object identity. Figure~\ref{fig:tsne_compositional} (Right) demonstrates a binary separation of the embedding space: one cluster containing all objects with wheels—regardless of whether they are vehicles or medical equipment—and another containing objects without wheels. This validates that our model can route attention to specific compositional tokens (e.g., the wheels) rather than the global object concept.
